Mynk — round 6: matrix with richer surfaces
4 palettes × 4 typography combos · 16 cells · ghost button + dashboard + AI panel · 2026-05-19

Same matrix, but with more surface area so the palettes actually differentiate.

You called it: round 5's compact cells made all four light modes look almost identical. That's because ~70% of each cell was cream surface and the only colored surfaces (small wordmark, tiny button, tiny tag) were too small to register the differences. The fix isn't more cards, it's more surfaces per card — specifically the ones where the palette differences actually live.

Five surfaces added or enlarged per cell, each one designed to expose a different token:

Brand stripe (left edge)
4px solid brand color on the left border of every cell. Always visible, always at scale. F1/F3 will show teal here; F2/F4 will show blue.
Ghost button
Second CTA next to "Start trial." Border uses border-strong — that's forest (#487D68) on F1/F3 vs blue-grey (#4A6680) on F2/F4. Hold your cursor over both buttons to read the contrast.
Mini dashboard
Two session rows with bordered dividers. Exposes border, text-muted, and the small AI tag (which uses accent-soft + accent-text).
Recording bar
The pulsing dot has a halo at 3× radius using accent-soft. F1/F2's bright coral halo is much more visible than F3/F4's muted coral halo.
AI summary panel
The largest accent-soft surface in each cell. This is where F1/F2's bright coral really separates from F3/F4's muted coral — it's a panel-sized splash of the accent family.
Body text weight (per V2/V3 column)
V2 columns render body text at Inter 400 + Fraunces 400 (lighter); V3 columns render at Inter 500 + Fraunces 500 (slightly heavier). Compare the sub-line under the heading, the small "Algebra II · 47 min" subtext in the dashboard rows, and the AI summary prose to see the body-weight delta cleanly between V2h and V3h columns.
If F1 and F2 still look "the same" after this, the answer is they essentially are. The only structural difference between them is brand color rotating from HSL 178° (teal) to 210° (blue) — same lightness, same saturation, same accent, same surface. That's an honest finding: the daughter's "blues feel softer" preference and your "Forest-Teal Bridge v2" preference may be picking the same palette wearing two hats.

What to look at, surface by surface

If you want to confirm a specific palette difference, here's where to focus your eyes:

F1 vs F2 (teal vs blue brand). Compare the left edge stripes — F1's teal vs F2's deeper blue. Then compare the ghost button borders — F1 forest #487D68 vs F2 blue-grey #4A6680. Both differences live in cool-family tokens; coral is identical between these two so the warmth signal is unchanged.

F1 vs F3 (bright coral vs muted coral). Look at the AI panel — F1's bright #F8E0D6 background is markedly warmer/peachier than F3's #F1DDD2 muted tint. Same for the primary button (#E27D60 vs #D08D7A) and the recording dot halo. Brand stripe is identical between F1 and F3 — it's purely a warmth-signal change.

F1 vs F4 (everything different at once). Compare F1 top-left to F4 bottom-right — every cool token is bluer and every warm token is quieter. This is the full softness pass. F4 should feel like the calmest cell on the page; the question is whether it still feels like Mynk.

If the F1↔F2 difference still feels too subtle even with all this surface area, that's the honest signal: blue and teal at the same lightness really are close. The daughter's "blues feel softer" preference may be a perceptual nudge of 10–15%, not a fundamental palette change. If so, either is a fine answer — pick whichever name reads better to the family ("Forest-Teal Bridge" vs "Twilight Bridge").
